Introduction

Motivation, purpose, and methods

Context: The opioid epidemic in United States has driven a rapid increase in drug overdose deaths. The CDC highlights that from 1999 to 2017, more than 700,000 people have died from a drug overdose in the U.S. and these deaths have increasingly involved opioids, representing approximately 68% of drug overdose deaths in 2017.

Audience: As public attention and media coverage has focused more and more on this epidemic, policymakers have faced increasing pressure to take steps to address the problem. It is important, therefore, that these politicians understand the nature of the epidemic and the various factors influencing its spread.

Topics: The sections of this report focus on three research areas that are relevant for potential policy interventions and are commonly discussed (though perhaps misunderstood) in the public discourse:

Drug Categories The changing landscape of classes of opioids causing overdose deaths
Prescription Opioids The relationship of the increase in overdose deaths with the prescription opioid market
Socio-Economic FactorsPoetntial geographical and socio-econmic factors to be considered for more targeted opioid intervention policies

Methods: The data underlying this study were compiled from multiple sources (please see Sources section). The primary purpose of this study is to explore this data via visualizations to raise and easily communicate findings of interest for policy makers.

NOTE: It is recommended to expand each visualization to full screen for optimal display. All plots are interactive.

Drug Categories

Opioid Overdose Deaths are Increasing Rapidly

Drug overdose deaths in general have increased by more than 4x since 1999. While alcohol-related deaths and non-opioid-related overdose deaths have increased across that span, the key driver has been opioid-related deaths. Of the 53,388 additional overdose deaths over that timespan, 39,550 (74%) were from opioid-related overdose deaths. The magnitude and direction of the opioid epidemic is clear and alarming.

Note that deaths here are defined according to ICD-10 Cause-of-Death codes. This limits the represented deaths here according to medical diagnoses at time of death. For alcohol-related deaths in particular, the CDC also makes estimates of alcohol-attributable deaths and report an average number of total annual alcohol-related deaths closer to 100,000 (50/50 acute vs chronic causes). This is important additional context when interpretting these results. Read more here.

Synthetic Opioids Most Dramatic Increase

If we drill down further into opioid-related overdose deaths, we can explore the data by class of opioid. There are 4 opioid classes:

Drug Class Description
Natural/Semisynthetic Opioids Natural opiate chemicals can be found in the opium plant (e.g. morphine) whereas semi-synthetic opioids are derived from these natural opiates (e.g. oxycondone). This class is made up primarily of prescription opioids (RxOs)
Heroin A particular semi-sythetic opioid that is distributed illicitly
Synthetic Opioids Opioids that are not derived from the opium plant, but are rather completely lab-created and mimic the effects of natural opiates (e.g. fentanyl)
Methadone A synthetic opioid that is widely used to treat opioid addiction as a means to weaken cravings and withdrawl symptoms

From 1999 to 2010, much of the increase in opioid-related overdose deaths was attributable to Natural/Semi-Synthetic Opioids.

Beginning in 2010 Heroin overdose deaths began increasing rapidly, as the increase in Natural/Semi-Synthetic overdose deaths slowed. This may coincide with the reformulation of OxyContin to stem RxO abuse and indicate a substitution effect but a confluence of factors, including an increased supply of heroin may all have affected this shift.

Then, in 2013, Synthetic Opioid overdose deaths began increasing even more extremely than either of the previous classes. Much of what is driving this increase is increased availability and potency of fentanyl. Because synthetic opioids can be made in a lab, and therefore do not rely on an agricultural supply chain (as with heroin), the illicit market has been flooded with synthetic opioids in recent years. Synthetic opioids are much more potent than heroin and prescription opioid medications, therefore drug users are less likely to be accustomed to proper dosing. In other cases fentanyl is being added to other illicit drugs to increase their potency which creates situations where users are not even aware of the content or strength of what they are taking. For clarity in comparison, opioid medications are often converted to a common unit of dosing measure: morphine-milligram-equivalent (MME) per day. While a standard dosage of RxOs for chronic pain treatment would be roughly 50 MME/day, the lethal does of fentanyl is onlyt one MG.

Prescription Opioids

Prescriptions Opioids Correlated with RxO Misuse & Overdose, but not Heroin

For this analysis we review the three most recent years of data to explore how recent prescribing rates correlate with levels of opioid misuse/abuse and overdose. The three most rercent years of data were used in particular to align with results from from the National Survey on Drug Use and Health (NDSUH), where recent survey results have included a question about misuse of prescription opioids (RxOs) ["misuse" defined as 'use in any way not directed by a doctor'] in addition to standard questions on participant illicit drug use, including heroin. Note also that state-level reporting for overdose deaths by class is limited and these results have been limited to those states with available data.
From the above plots we saw that from 2016 to 2017 overdose deaths from both heroin and natural/semi-synthetic opioids were similar in magnitude and steady. This makes comparison against RxO prescribing rates particularly insightful.

One common story shared from the opioid epidemic is that of people prescribed RxOs who then begin to misuse and abuse their prescriptions. These people may then become opioid dependent or addicted and potentially divert to taking an illicit opioid, such as heroin.

The below scatter plot matrix includes state-year-level observations over the years 2015-2017 comparing prescribing rates against overdose deaths from RxOs and heroin (on the left axis) and RxO misuse and heroin use (on the bottom axis).

What we see is that states in years with higher RxO prescribing rates have a weak positive relationship with RxO misuse and overdose deaths, but not with heroin use or overdose.
Heroin does, however, show correlation between use and overdose deaths.

Prescription Opioids Not Correlated with Overall Drug Overdose Deaths

Similar to the above plot we then take a step back to look at correlation between prescribing rates and all general drug overdose deaths.

What we see is much less clear than the above plots narrowly focused on RxO overdose: the slope of the relationship has been decreasing the past 3 years to the point of being completely unrelated in 2017. While this does not necessarily decouple a relationship between high prescribing rates and overdose deaths, what it does show is that 1) there are likely many factors affecting overdose death levels (not just RxO prescribing rates) and 2) the trends may be shifting. The opioid epidemic has put increased scrutiny on the RxO industry - the shifts toward illicit heroin and fentanyl may be indicative of a change in the epidemic and policymakers should be congnizant of this.
